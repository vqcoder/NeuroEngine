# AlphaEngine Homepage Strategic Rewrite

---

## 1. DIAGNOSIS: Current Site Audit

### What's Working

- **The "One Customer's Week" narrative is the strongest asset on the site.** The Monday-through-Sunday breakdown of a single customer hitting seven disconnected touchpoints is concrete, emotional, and immediately recognizable to any CMO or CRO. This is rare in B2B SaaS — most competitors describe this problem abstractly. Keep and strengthen it.
- **The tone is premium without being stiff.** The writing avoids startup hype and reads as strategic. It speaks at the CMO/board level without dumbing down.
- **The proof metrics are specific and credible.** 36.8% view rate, 140–280% incremental ROAS vs. 6.4% last-click, and 3.4x revenue growth ($1.1B to $3.8B) — these are concrete and memorable. They don't overclaim.
- **The role-based targeting is smart.** Listing six buyer personas (CMO, CRO, VP Lifecycle, Head of Product, CS/Support, CEO/PE) signals enterprise awareness.
- **"Request a Pilot" as the primary CTA is strong.** It's lower friction than "Book a Demo" and signals that AlphaEngine is confident enough to let results speak.
- **The incrementality / GeoX measurement framing is a genuine differentiator.** Positioning against last-click attribution is correct and timely.

### What's Confusing

- **The site blends two fundamentally different problems into one undifferentiated narrative.** Customer relationship management (retention, memory, orchestration) and new customer acquisition (targeting, creative optimization, attribution) are presented as a single solution. A CMO reading this doesn't know whether AlphaEngine is a CRM intelligence layer, a media optimization platform, or both. This is the critical structural problem.
- **The five-step framework (Connect → Memory → Value → Orchestrate → Prove) mixes Part A and Part B logic.** Steps 1–4 are about managing existing relationships. Step 5 (Prove What Worked) is about measurement and attribution — which serves both, but especially matters for acquisition. These are fused together.
- **The hero headline ("AI-Native Customer Engagement Operating System") is vague.** "Customer Engagement Operating System" sounds like another CDP or journey orchestration tool. It doesn't communicate the dual-problem thesis or the strategic ambition.
- **The proof metrics live in one block with no clear linkage to specific capabilities.** The 36.8% view rate is about creative optimization (Part B). The 3.4x revenue growth could be either. The 140–280% incremental ROAS is about measurement (Part B). None of these prove the relationship memory / orchestration thesis (Part A).
- **There is no clear marketecture or architecture visual.** The five steps are described in text but not visualized as a system. Enterprise buyers expect to see an architecture diagram.
- **The transition from "here's the problem" to "here's the solution" is abrupt.** The customer journey story is vivid, but the solution section jumps into product features without clearly framing why this architecture is different from what they already have.

### What to Keep

1. The "One Customer's Week" journey narrative — strengthen it
2. The premium strategic tone
3. The three proof metrics (36.8%, 140–280%, 3.4x)
4. The role-based buyer framing
5. "Request a Pilot" as the primary CTA
6. The incrementality / measurement positioning
7. The organizational cost framing (retention, media waste, missed expansion)

### What Must Change

1. Split the homepage into two clear growth narratives: Part A (grow existing) and Part B (acquire new)
2. Rewrite the hero to frame the dual-problem thesis
3. Build a dedicated Part A section with marketecture (Customer Connect → Memory Banks → Intelligence → Orchestration → Truth)
4. Build a dedicated Part B section with product suite (Targeting, Bio Optimizer, Attribution)
5. Add an explicit bridge section connecting Part A insight to Part B advantage
6. Place proof metrics under the correct part of the story
7. Add architecture/diagram concepts to make the system tangible
8. Reframe the hero away from "engagement operating system" and toward "customer growth operating system"

### SECTION 2: THE BROKEN CUSTOMER JOURNEY

**Headline:**
One Customer. Seven Touchpoints. Zero Coordination.

**Subhead:**
Your customer experiences one relationship. Your company operates through disconnected systems. Here's what that actually looks like.

**Body:**

**Monday.** She hits a bug in the product. She reports it through in-app feedback. The product team logs it in their backlog tool. Marketing doesn't know. Support doesn't know. CRM doesn't know.

**Tuesday.** She calls support about the same bug. The agent has no record of her in-app report. She explains the issue from scratch. Support logs a ticket in their system. It doesn't connect to the product backlog or her CRM record.

**Wednesday.** Marketing sends her an upsell email for the premium tier — the one that includes the feature that's currently broken for her. She doesn't open it. She screenshots it and sends it to a colleague with a frustrated comment.

**Thursday.** She receives a customer satisfaction survey. She rates the company a 3 out of 10. The research team logs the score. Nobody connects it to the open support ticket, the broken feature, or the badly timed upsell.

**Friday.** An automated review request hits her inbox. She's still waiting on her support ticket. She ignores it. Her engagement score drops internally, but nobody knows why.

**Saturday.** She sees a retargeting ad for the product she already owns. She's paying for it. She's frustrated with it. Now she's being sold it again. Her trust drops further.

**Sunday.** A renewal notice arrives with a generic retention offer — a discount on something she doesn't need. The cancellation process is easier than the support process was.

**Result:** A customer worth $4,200 in lifetime value is gone. Reacquisition cost: ~$380. The company doesn't know what happened, because no system connected the signals.

**Counterpoint (callout):**
Now imagine the same week — with shared memory. Support knows about the bug report before she calls. Marketing suppresses the upsell. The survey triggers a service recovery workflow. The review request is held. The retargeting ad is paused. The renewal offer is personalized to her actual situation. She stays. She upgrades three months later.

**That's the difference between channel automation and relationship intelligence.**

### SECTION 4: PART A — THE COMPANY PROBLEM

**Headline:**
Fragmentation Doesn't Just Frustrate Customers. It Bleeds the Business.

**Subhead:**
When systems don't share context, companies miss the signals that drive retention, expansion, and long-term value.

**Body (structured as problem pairs):**

**Retention.**

**Revenue Expansion.**

**Advocacy.**
Your most satisfied customers are your best growth channel — but you can't activate them if you don't know who they are, when they're receptive, or what made them successful. Advocacy programs built on static segments underperform.

**Media Efficiency.**
You're paying to reacquire customers you already have. You're retargeting people who are frustrated. You're suppressing nobody. Without customer memory informing media, acquisition budgets subsidize retention failures.

**Real-Time Understanding.**
The most important question in customer growth isn't "what happened?" It's "what is happening right now — and what should we do about it?" Fragmented systems can answer the first question, slowly. They cannot answer the second one at all.

### SECTION 5: PART A — THE MARKETECTURE

**Headline:**
The AlphaEngine Relationship Engine.

**Subhead:**
Shared memory. Contextual intelligence. Coordinated action. Measured truth.

**Body:**
The Relationship Engine is not a single product — it's an architecture. Six layers that work together to turn disconnected customer touchpoints into a unified growth system.

---

**Layer 1: Customer Connect**

*The routing and signal-ingestion layer.*

Customer Connect wires together the systems that already touch your customers — product events, marketing engagement, service interactions, sales and CS activity, surveys and research, billing and renewal signals, and media and audience data.

Its job: connect systems, normalize signals, resolve identity, and create one live relationship stream that every downstream layer reads from.

This is not another integration middleware. It's the foundation that makes shared memory possible.

---

**Layer 2: Contextual Memory Banks**

*Not one generic customer profile. A set of purpose-built memory systems.*

Most platforms promise a "unified customer profile." What they deliver is a flat record with too many fields and no context. AlphaEngine takes a different approach: purpose-built memory banks that preserve the right context for the right decisions.

- **Product Memory** — What the customer uses, how they use it, where they hit friction, what they haven't discovered yet.
- **Service Memory** — What they've asked for help with, how it was resolved, what's still open, how their service experience has evolved.
- **Engagement Memory** — How they respond to outreach across channels, what content resonates, what timing works, what they ignore.
- **Commercial Memory** — What they've purchased, what they've been offered, how pricing has changed, what their spending pattern reveals.
- **Lifecycle Memory** — Where they are in their journey, what job they're trying to get done, how their needs are changing over time.
- **Relationship Memory** — The overall health of the relationship — integrating signals from every other memory bank into a living assessment of trust, satisfaction, and potential.

Each memory bank is continuously updated. Every team and channel reads from the same set. The result: when marketing acts, it knows what support just handled. When sales reaches out, it knows what the product data reveals. When service responds, it knows what the customer was trying to accomplish.

---

**Layer 3: Relationship Intelligence Engine**

*The scoring and decision layer.*

Memory without intelligence is just storage. The Relationship Intelligence Engine turns contextual memory into actionable scores and recommendations.

- **Valuation Score** — What is this customer worth today?
- **Customer Potential Score** — What could this customer be worth?
- **Next Best Action Score** — What should we do right now — and what should we not do?
- **Jobs To Be Done by Lifecycle Stage** — What is this customer trying to accomplish, and how does that shift as they mature?
- **Receptivity Score** — Is this the right moment to engage, or the right moment to be silent?
- **Friction / Risk Score** — Is this relationship at risk, and what's driving it?

---

**Layer 4: AI Personalizer**

*Connected intelligence across the entire lifecycle.*

The AI Personalizer sits on top of the Intelligence Engine and Memory Banks. It understands the customer across product, marketing, and service — at any point in the lifecycle — and determines not just what to say, but whether to say anything at all.

This is the layer that prevents the Wednesday upsell email to the Monday bug reporter. It's also the layer that identifies the quiet customer who just hit a usage milestone and is ready for an expansion conversation.

---

**Layer 5: Orchestration Layer**

*The right action, in the right channel, at the right time — or deliberate silence.*

The Orchestration Layer activates decisions across every channel the customer touches:

- Email and lifecycle messaging
- In-product messaging and nudges
- Customer success and support workflows
- Sales and renewal motions
- Survey and research follow-up
- Suppression logic — deliberately holding back when engagement would hurt
- Paid media retargeting, exclusion, and suppression
- Pricing and offer triggers

Orchestration is not about sending more messages. It's about coordinating the right messages — and knowing when not to send any.

---

**Layer 6: Truth Layer**

*Measure whether the relationship is getting healthier — not just whether the message was sent.*

Most engagement platforms optimize for activity metrics: opens, clicks, conversions. The Truth Layer optimizes for relationship health:

- Engagement depth — not volume
- Customer satisfaction trajectory
- Retention and churn prevention
- Cross-sell and upsell conversion
- Advocacy activation
- Pricing power and willingness to pay
- Incremental revenue — what actually moved because of the action taken

The Truth Layer closes the loop. It feeds back into the Intelligence Engine and Memory Banks, making every subsequent decision smarter than the last.
